Market Participants

Core Concept

Financial markets are driven by different types of participants, each with distinct roles and behaviors.

MARKET ECOSYSTEM │ ├── Retail Investors (Individuals) │ ├── Trade through brokers │ └── Typically <$1M accounts │ ├── Institutional Investors │ ├── Mutual Funds ($100M+) │ ├── Pension Funds ($B+) │ └── Hedge Funds (Sophisticated) │ └── Market Makers ├── Provide liquidity └── Profit from bid-ask spreads

Key Insights

  • Institutional dominance: Control ~75% of daily trading volume
  • Market makers: Continuously quote buy/sell prices to facilitate trading
  • Retail traders: Often at disadvantage due to slower execution

Order Types Explained

Core Concept

Different order types allow traders to specify how and when their trades should be executed.

ORDER TYPE HIERARCHY │ ├── MARKET ORDERS │ ├── Execute immediately │ ├── No price guarantee │ └── Best for liquidity │ ├── LIMIT ORDERS │ ├── Set price boundary │ ├── Buy limit ≤ target │ └── Sell limit ≥ target │ └── STOP ORDERS ├── Triggered by price ├── Stop-loss: Limit losses └── Stop-buy: Enter breakouts

Practical Examples

Scenario 1: You want to buy AAPL now at whatever price is available → Market order

Scenario 2: You'll only buy AAPL if it drops to $150 → Buy limit order at $150

Scenario 3: You want to sell if AAPL falls below $145 → Stop-loss at $145

Order Selection

Behavioral Finance Basics

Core Concept

Psychological factors often cause investors to make irrational decisions that contradict traditional finance theory.

COMMON COGNITIVE BIASES │ ├── Loss Aversion │ ├── Pain of loss > joy of gain │ └── Leads to holding losers too long │ ├── Herding │ ├── Following the crowd │ └── Buying high, selling low │ └── Anchoring ├── Fixating on purchase price └── Reluctance to sell below "anchor"

Real-World Examples

Dot-com bubble: Herding into overvalued tech stocks

2008 crisis: Anchoring to pre-crash home prices

COVID crash: Loss aversion causing panic selling

Bias Identification

An investor refuses to sell a stock at $45 because they bought it at $60. This is:

Herding
Anchoring
Confirmation bias

What's the best antidote to behavioral biases?

Following financial news
Sticking to a written plan
Trusting gut feelings

Candlestick Patterns

Core Concept

Candlestick patterns visually represent price action and often signal potential trend reversals or continuations.

COMMON PATTERNS │ ├── BULLISH REVERSAL │ ├── Hammer: Long lower wick │ └── Engulfing: Large green candle │ └── BEARISH REVERSAL ├── Shooting Star: Long upper wick └── Evening Star: Three-candle top

Pattern Psychology

Hammer: Sellers push price down but buyers regain control

Engulfing: Complete dominance of buyers over sellers

Doji: Indecision (open ≈ close)

Arbitrage Strategies

Core Concept

Arbitrage exploits price discrepancies across markets to generate risk-free profits, requiring sophisticated technology.

TRIANGULAR ARBITRAGE (Forex) USD → EUR → GBP → USD │ │ │ $1.00 €0.85 £0.75 │ │ │ └──────┴──────┘ Profit if loop returns > initial USD

Execution Requirements

  • Latency: Sub-millisecond execution
  • Capital: Large positions needed
  • Monitoring: Real-time price feeds
